## 2. The Cycling to Work in -6C Story

4 years ago, I'd cycle 40 mins to work in -6°C weather.

I couldn’t afford a £2.38 bus fare.

Now?

I’m on mat leave, stress free.
My business runs without me.
I’ve got 12 amazing contractors.

Some people start a business for…

- Ego, glory and status
- Awards and Forbes features
- Flashy cars and million-pound exits

Not me.

I started mine...

To not walk around a supermarket with a calculator.
To put whatever I wanted in my Amazon basket.
To get an Uber (+ not walk home alone at night)

For me, it's always been about:

Peace. Security. Freedom.

Just a few years ago…

- I lived in a damp bedsit where the roof caved in.
- Dinner was 49p pot noodles most nights.
- Fruit was a luxury I couldn't afford.

Am I ashamed?

HELL NO!

As an immigrant to this country…
Who has never had any financial help…
Whose arts career ended overnight after a stroke at 22…

I had to rebuild my life in my mid-20s.

My biggest anxiety wasn’t “How do I hit 7-figures?”

It was:

💭 “Will I make rent this month?”
💭 “What can I afford to eat this week?”
💭 “If I get the bus, what will I have to sacrifice?”

But now?

After years of hard work
(and plenty of ups & downs)…
I can give my family the life we deserve.

So if you’re in that place right now...

Just trying to survive…

Please know: It doesn’t last forever.

And I believe in you.

SO MUCH ❤️

## 4. The ADHD Meds Shortage Story

I've run out of my ADHD meds.

And I'm struggling now.

I'm 39, a husband & father of 2.
An ADHD coach & psychotherapist.
I coach people to work _with_ their ADHD brains.

And even I’m struggling right now.

All because of a national ADHD meds shortage.

For me, it means:

👎🏼 Bouncing between tabs like my brain’s on shuffle
👎🏼 Picking up my phone 10x more than usual
👎🏼 Structure feeling like it’s made of sand

ADHD meds aren't just a bonus.

They’re a lifeline.

They can help us:

✔️ Modulate norepinephrine levels
✔️ Strengthen inhibitory control
✔️ Regulate intense feelings
✔️ Improve cognitive flexibility
✔️ Boost dopamine (interest)
✔️ Support working memory
✔️ Reduce cortical “noise”

When support is pulled away suddenly
(with no warning, no replacement)...

It’s not as simple as just "pushing through".

It's destabilising.

What worries me most?

I know ADHDers who are being:

- Told they’re “falling behind”
- Put on performance plans
- Laid off

...while being DENIED the very thing that supports their executive functioning.

The thing that gives them equity in the workplace.

It’s immoral. It’s ableist.
And it’s being ignored.

What I’ve had to do:

🧩 Rebuild my whole day
🎮 Gamify boring tasks
🧘 Add more breaks, movement, novelty
💡 Plan my energy, not just my time
🫶 Be way, way kinder to myself

Even with all the tools I have - it’s still hard.

Really f\*cking hard.

And my heart breaks for those in less privileged situations.

So if you're feeling:

→ More scattered
→ More emotional
→ More distracted
→ Less focused
→ Less regulated
→ Less “productive”

You’re not lazy. You’re not failing.

You’re missing a piece of your scaffolding.

If you manage or lead ADHDers at work...

Please show some understanding.

This shortage isn’t just about meds.

It’s also about dignity.

🧡 If this hits home - I see you.
You’re doing better than you think.

Let’s keep talking about it.

Together.

## 5. The Hustling Dad Story

My dad would say: “Once I retire, I’ll rest.”
But he died at 56 years old.
He never made it.

Dad loved his job.

He was a Headteacher.

The kind of man who gave everything he had to his students (and everyone loved him).

But work took everything else.

His evenings.
His weekends.
His family time.

At 6pm, he’d come home after an extra long day, have dinner, then open his bag again by 7:30pm. Marking until 10pm.

Saturday would come.
He’d fall asleep on the sofa.
Paperwork stacked by his side.

We'd joke it was 'recovery'.

Still, he was the best dad ever ❤️

I'm not angry at him.

I’m angry at what he was taught to believe.

→ “Push hard now and then rest later.”

He did everything right.

But he never stopped.

And when he finally got close to the finish line, it got snatched away.

Listen... I’m not anti-work.

I’m anti “work takes your whole life.”

• You CAN get promoted without 60 hr weeks.
• You CAN hit targets & be present at home.
• You CAN scale income and still enjoy life.

But we overwork so much now that any form of balance feels 'rebellious'.

Almost 'selfish’.

But there's no 'pride' in being 'too busy' for your kids.

Or 'too tired' to actually enjoy your life.

Work pressure isn’t going anywhere.
Targets. KPIs. Responsibilities.
They will always be there.

But how you respond to that pressure can change.

If work is stealing:

- Your relationships
- Your headspace
- Your energy
- Your time

That’s not “just how it is.”

That’s a set of systems you CAN transform.

- By setting clearer boundaries
- Having more structured routines
- Self-advocating confidently

Because if you don't...

You risk losing your entire life to your employer.

Please don’t make that mistake.

## 6. The Distracted Diner Story

Last night, I went out for dinner. At the table next to us, a man got up to take a call. 25 minutes later, his daughter (maybe 7 or 8??) was still there.

It was 7:30pm ish.

I could tell he didn’t want to take the call.
He hesitated for a second.

But he took it anyway.

And this girl (still in school uniform) sat there, moving her chips around her plate.

I watched for a while.

Every few minutes, she looked up at the door, twisting her paper straw between her fingers.

My heart broke for her.

Eventually, dad came back.

But he didn't sit down.
Just got his coat and said:
"Sorry love, it was work. Are you done?"

His food just stayed there.
Cold and untouched.

He paid the bill and left.

My partner and I sat there stunned.

I can't stop thinking about it.

The saddest part is that this happens all the time and we've just become numb to it.

We tell ourselves:

"It can't be helped."
"We all have to do it."
"It's important."

But “one quick call” turns into another 30 minutes of stolen time that you can never get back.

And it’s never just one, is it?

• It’s a Teams ping at bedtime.

• A “can you just...?” at 10am on Saturday.

• An email you answer from the sofa - “it’ll only take a second" - you tell your partner.
Meanwhile…

Kids go quiet.
Dinner goes cold.
Partners give up waiting.

We live in a culture where being available 24/7 to your employer is normalised.

Even PRAISED!

It's dressed up as "commitment".

But to who?

I wanted to lean over and say...

"Your boss doesn’t need you at 7:30pm. Your little girl does!"

(My husband stopped me haha).

The fact is... work will take every inch you give it.

So if you never give it edges, it will quietly take your life too.

In ten years, I wonder...

→ How that girl will remember her dad.
→ How he’ll remember himself.

One day, your child might be the person sitting at that table in a restaurant, eating their chips.

I hope you’re sitting there with them.

## 8. The Return to Work Story

After seven months of maternity leave, I nervously asked for a meeting with my boss.

Me: “Can I work from home one day a week?”

Boss: "...No."

Me: "But I..."

Boss: “It’s our policy. We don’t make exceptions.
We expect our leadership team 120% in office.”

I wanted to work one day a week from home.

So I could:

- Save 90 minutes of commuting
- Do deep work without office interruptions
- Catch my breath from constant travel

Just one day.

My boss said no because of “policy”.

I persevered for a couple of years, then I resigned.

I loved the job. And the people.

But after years of sacrificing my mental wellbeing to commute to an office (national and international), sit behind a desk and work 8 hours a day there (for 5 days a week)...

I realised how unsustainable it had become.

I had given my all to that company.

Late nights. Unpaid overtime. Exceeding KPIs.

So it felt deeply disappointing.

The hardest part was realising I wasn’t trusted.

Ironically, just one day working from home a week would have…

Improved output
Reduced my stress
Benefited my employer

The world of work is changing.

Company attitudes towards flexibility need to keep up.

How many talented people are we still losing to “policy”?

P.S. Now I run my own company and I work where I’m most productive.

Today, that's my writing desk.

## 10. The Breastfeeding Client Call Story

Yesterday, I breastfed my baby on a client call.

Nobody batted an eyelid.

Fabulous.

Because I want to normalise this.

When you don’t have childcare and your husband’s working miles away, you adapt.

Here’s what happened...

I put Murphy down for a nap.
Opened a Zoom call with a client.
2 minutes in, the baby monitor goes off.

She's screaming her tiny lungs out.

So I did what mums do best.

I made it work.

Camera off.
Mic on.
Baby on b\*\*b.

I told the client what was happening.

Then carried on the 40-minute call.

It was no drama.

Everyone got what they needed.

My client got her speaking coaching.
My baby got fed.

And afterwards, I just thought...

How incredible that I could do that.

(FYI: I’m self-employed.)

But also…

How RIDICULOUS that so many women can’t.

Because in most workplaces?

Even acknowledging your baby needs feeding would be labelled “unprofessional.”

Cue the comments:

👉🏼 “Should she really be doing that?”
👉🏼 “It’s company policy to keep your camera on.”
👉🏼 “That’s not appropriate for a meeting.”

Oh, PLEASE. 🙄

We’re all adults.

If the work gets done and no one’s disrupted...

What’s the issue?

And yes, I know…

This isn’t a very “LinkedIn” post.

Some people will roll their eyes.

But frankly, I don't care.

Not everyone has a nanny.
Not everyone has family nearby.
Not everyone has a partner to share the load.

And those people shouldn’t be punished for it.

P.S. Have you ever had to parent mid-meeting or improvise during a workday?

Drop a 💜 if you have.

It’s time we normalised what working motherhood actually looks like.
