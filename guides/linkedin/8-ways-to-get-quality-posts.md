# 8 Ways to Get Quality LinkedIn Post Ideas

Most people don't struggle with LinkedIn because they "aren't creative." They struggle because they don't know where to look for good stories in their own lives.

This guide shows you how to extract compelling, shareable content from your real experiences - your history, decisions, mistakes, conversations and identity.

Each strategy is a practical way to help you notice moments that already carry emotion, contrast, and meaning and turn them into stories that help people get to know you, trust you and remember you.

The focus is on building influence by showing your thinking, your journey and the human context behind what you do.

---

## Strategy 1: Scroll Through Old Photos

Look through WhatsApp / Messenger chats, Instagram story archives, photos that your family and friends took of you, email attachments from years ago (include old emails you might not use anymore), physical photo albums, your current camera roll, etc.

For each photo, write down:

1. When was this?
2. Where were you living + working at the time?
3. What feelings come to the surface?
4. What was going on in your life then?

The goal here is to find interesting moments you can pinpoint, zoom in on and tell an interesting story about. Show contrast (then vs now / before vs after). How has life changed? What did the moment teach you? Is there an inspirational/motivational message in there?

Use the picture as the starting point (the visual hook) for your post.

---

## Strategy 2: Create a Life Timeline

Braindump your life like a messy CV.

- First job (salary + responsibilities + job title)
- Education (what you did/was it helpful?)
- Career pivots, failures, layoffs
- Health journeys/diagnoses
- Big relationship shifts
- Financial milestones

Add dates, context and numbers to each, e.g. "First job, age 16. £4.50 an hour. Retail. Standing for 8 hours. Hated it. Learned how customers actually behave."

Each entry could be (1) a "get to know me" story, (2) a lesson someone else needs to hear and (3) a prompt for others to share their experiences and start discussions. These posts show where you came from to amplify the impact of your messaging and root your POV in real-life experiences, so people believe you've truly lived the things you say.

---

## Strategy 3: Dive Into Your Biggest Mistakes

List your biggest life and business mistakes. Examples:

- Ignoring your health
- Bad career decisions
- Staying in the wrong job too long
- Chasing superficial signs of success
- Launching something too early (or too late)
- Taking bad advice / trusting the wrong people

Once you have your list, pick one mistake and write down: when did this happen - age, year, stage of life or business, what was your thinking, what were you hoping for, what were the warning signs you ignored, what did you lose because of it?

Important: micro details matter, e.g. the financial cost of that loss.

Now decide how to frame the post. Each mistake can become:

- A "Here's what I got wrong" story
- A "This mistake cost me X" post
- A "I wish someone had told me this earlier" reflection
- A lesson disguised as a personal confession

The key is to anchor your message in the lived moment. Instead of saying something generic like "Always trust your gut," write: "At 24, I ignored my gut and missed out on a life-changing promotion."

These posts work because they show your human side, that you made real decisions and braved the consequences and that your perspective is earned (not copied). People appreciate transparency (it makes you more likeable, relatable and trustworthy).

---

## Strategy 4: (Re-)Introduce Yourself

An intro post is a short story-based way to help people understand who you are, why you're here, your life arc (key moments that stand out and are interesting), so they can place you in their head and connect your future posts to a real human.

This is not CV writing. Don't oversell yourself or talk just about your expertise.

Package your story into 6-8 bullet points or dates that show where you started vs where you are (with a few interesting moments in between). Include a mix of themes/moments in life.

The goal is to create intrigue and make someone think, "oh you seem interesting, I want to know more" (so they follow you). A strong intro post gives people emotional context and invites others to engage with you more openly. Include a well-lit, face-to-camera picture.

## Strategy 5: Find Moments of Rebellion

Scan your past for times you were underestimated, doubted or discouraged.

- Have you ever been told you can't do something?
- Have you ever been given advice you ignored?
- Have you ever gone against the grain?

Open a document and write down these situations, e.g. job rejections, being passed over for promotion, being told you lacked experience or credibility, choosing a non-traditional path, losing a client, choosing not to follow 'traditional' success.

For each moment, add concrete detail: how old you were, where you were working or living, what the expectation was and what made the situation feel stacked against you at the time.

Then write what you did next and the outcome. How did you get there? What did you do? How can others also do this? Each underdog moment can become a post about proving people wrong, challenging narrow definitions of success or championing people like you.

## Strategy 6: Convert Calendars Into Content

Treat your diary/calendar as a record of lived experience. Go back over a defined period - a week, a month, a quarter, or even a year - and look at everything: calls, meetings, launches, deadlines, trips, workouts, health appointments, social plans, holidays, etc.

Write down what you did (things that went well, things that didn't and things that were fun, unexpected or notable). Note projects delivered, clients signed, milestones hit, or progress made, but also include missed targets, ideas that flopped, decisions you'd rethink, etc.

Then package this content into 'build in public' posts. These 2 things:

1. Remind people what you do and why you're good at it - creates momentum and MOVEMENT around your brand
2. Makes you feel trustworthy - you bake relatability into your content - help other people open up about their journey (warts and all)

---

## Strategy 7: Document 'Moments' as You Go

Don't wait for a great content idea to just appear. Document things in real time.

Pay attention to anything that triggers a reaction in you - frustration, relief, surprise, validation, joy, etc. This could be a DM, an email, a comment you hear on the train - anything!

When something sparks an emotional response, either screenshot it immediately or send yourself a voicenote, brain-dumping your thoughts. Do this before you forget.

When you come to write posts, go through your screenshots and voice notes. Ask: Who else experiences this? What's the shared pain point? Who or what is the "villain" here - bad advice, unrealistic expectations, broken systems, unspoken rules, or outdated norms?

The post doesn't need to expose anyone (but it can playfully set up a 'villain'); consider how to turn it into commentary on that topic or a lesson for others to learn. These posts resonate because they are rooted in reality and help validate others who share this experience.

---

## Strategy 8: Leverage Your POV / Identity

This starts with deliberately identifying the parts of your life that influence how you work, think, and experience the world.

Write down the roles, identities, or lived experiences that meaningfully shape your day-to-day reality, such as being a working parent, an ADHDer, neurodivergent, a woman in a male-dominated industry, a career switcher, a first-generation professional, a solo founder, a person with a chronic injury or someone balancing work with caring responsibilities.

Next, turn these identities into idea prompts by asking:

- When have you felt misunderstood?
- What standard advice does not apply to you?
- What unique challenges have you faced because of it?
- When has this identity made work harder than expected?
- When did you have to work around systems that weren't designed with you in mind?

Write down concrete moments rather than general feelings - including where you were, what happened, who was involved and what assumption or rule you were pushing against.

Then look for the lesson embedded in that moment:

- What does it reveal about success, work, ambition, productivity, or balance that others might relate to?
- How did you challenge or overcome this barrier? What did it mean for you? Try to spotlight things people outside of this identity might not understand.
- What should people do differently? What would have helped you? How can society as a collective do better?

These posts work because they humanise you instantly. Identity-led content is about using lived experience to create a distinct point of view that only you can offer. This helps people get to know you, how you think and where you're operating from (on a much deeper level!!). It also helps you advocate for others in that position, which is a great way to drive traction and build community.
