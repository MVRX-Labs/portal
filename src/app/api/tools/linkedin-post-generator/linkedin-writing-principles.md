## 1. Hook Mechanics

The hook is the first 2 lines of a LinkedIn post — the only text visible before "...see more." Its sole job is to stop the scroll and earn a click.

### What makes a hook work

- **Tension**: Create a gap between what the reader expects and what you say. Curiosity gaps, contradictions, surprising facts.
- **Specificity**: Concrete numbers, names, timeframes beat vague claims. "50,000 scenes in 48 hours" beats "lots of traction."
- **Brevity**: Each hook line should be punchy. Max 12 words per line. Two lines total.
- **Pattern interrupt**: Challenge a belief, flip a common narrative, or open with vulnerability.

### Hook categories that work

- **Belief breaker**: "I used to [belief]. Until I learned [lesson]."
- **Myth buster**: "The biggest lie in [industry]? [Myth to debunk]."
- **Vulnerability opener**: "I thought [misconception]. But [mistake to avoid]."
- **Us vs them**: "Most people: [stereotypical advice]. Me: [less common advice]."
- **Proof-led**: "Here's how I helped [person] get [result]. Without [X]. Without [Y]. Just this."
- **Challenge stack**: "[Challenge 1]? [Challenge 2]? [Challenge 3]? Here's how [desirable transformation]."
- **Contrarian**: "Most people do [thing]. Here's [better approach]."
- **Transformation**: "I used to [old action]. Now [better results from new action]."
- **Results-first**: "I did [simple action]. It [desirable stat-based result]."
- **Reframe**: "You don't need [thing]. You need [real need]."
- **Comparison flip**: "People think [common thing] helps [goal]. But [alternative] is 10x better."
- **Timeline contrast**: "2024: [negative state]. 2025: [positive state]."
- **Journey contrast**: "1st [action]: [negative outcome]. 7th [action]: [positive outcome]."
- **Secret reveal**: "What's the secret to [desirable outcome]? This is what DID vs DIDN'T work for me."
- **Struggle to success**: "I was stuck in [negative state] for [time]. Until I [story-led action]."
- **Behind the scenes**: "Everyone saw the [launch/win/success]. No one saw the [struggle behind it]."
- **Stop doing**: "Stop doing [common mistake]. Do [action]. Let me explain..."
- **Forget the old**: "Forget [traditional method]. Here's how I [result] through [new method]."

### Hook rules

- Two lines only
- Maximum 12 words per line
- No fluff words — every word earns its place
- The hook should work even if the reader never clicks "see more"
- Adapt the hook pattern to match the client's voice and topic — the templates are starting points, not fill-in-the-blank forms

## 2. Viral Storytelling Checklist

Use this as a quality check against every post body you write. The best posts incorporate at least 8 of these 12 principles:

1. **Introduce a villain** — A common enemy (a system, belief, experience) that your reader finds frustrating. Call it out in the first few lines. Root it in a specific story, not abstract commentary.

2. **Twist the knife on the villain** — Describe what the villain costs your reader. What does it look like? Feel like? What happens if they don't fix it? The more vivid, the more relatable.

3. **Create drama in the opening lines** — Tease conflict, struggle, or risk early. People gravitate towards stakes. Give just enough to keep them reading.

4. **Set up a situational story** — Illustrate your point visually through words. Anchor it in time and place with specific details: the location, the date, the setting. Do this in the hook if possible.

5. **Establish a strong character** — Never speak in a vacuum. Build the argument through a specific person (hero or villain) so it feels tangible, immersive, and relatable.

6. **Tease an A to B transformation** — Point A is negative (where you/reader were). Point B is aspirational (where you/reader are now). Open up the gap. Highlight where you started with unflattering detail so the success feels earned.

7. **Call out ridiculousness** — Take a universal frustration and name it. Be the spokesperson for what most people think but are afraid to say. Present a simple, obvious alternative.

8. **Use numeric manipulation** — Reframe numbers to magnify impact. Break figures down to daily costs, stretch over time, convert to personal terms. Specific numbers (not round ones) increase believability.

9. **Make it accessible to the masses** — Choose broad, universal themes: failure, ambition, confidence, careers, leadership, mental health. Then differentiate with a unique entry point: a bold opinion, personal story, specific experience.

10. **Make the reader feel instantly seen** — Agitate a close-to-home struggle. Don't talk "about" their problem — describe what it feels like to live it. Poke the things they've experienced.

11. **Create disruption mid-post** — Include an unexpected shift, abrupt change of tone, or contrarian thinking to keep readers engaged. Each line needs to "rehook" the reader.

12. **Make it feel like something you've experienced** — It should read like a flow of uninterrupted thought. Avoid complex words or jargon. Use everyday language. A story rooted in your reality is more powerful than generic advice.

## 3. Body Writing Principles

### LinkedIn-Optimised Body

This version is structured to maximise algorithmic reach and engagement.

**Structure:**

- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences max)
- Generous white space between paragraphs
- One idea per paragraph
- Build momentum line by line — each line should make the reader want the next
- Use em-dashes for conversational rhythm
- Include a "disruption" moment mid-post (a shift, a twist, a surprising detail)
- End with a clear CTA that invites engagement (question, invitation to share, or direct ask)
- 3-5 relevant hashtags at the end
- Total length: 150-300 words for a standard post

**What to include:**

- Specific details from the source material (names, numbers, timeframes, quotes)
- A narrative arc: setup → tension → resolution/insight
- At least one direct quote if available in the source material
- An emotional throughline — the reader should feel something

**What to avoid:**

- Generic corporate phrases ("thrilled to announce", "excited to share", "proud to")
- Starting with "I'm" or "We're"
- Jargon or buzzwords unless they're genuinely part of the brand voice
- Walls of text
- Lists longer than 5 items (use storytelling instead)
- Ending with "What do you think?" (overused — find more creative CTAs)

### Humanised Body

This version prioritises authenticity and emotional resonance over algorithmic structure.

**Characteristics:**

- Reads like a conversation or journal entry
- Longer, more flowing sentences are acceptable
- Less structured — can be more stream-of-consciousness
- Emotional honesty takes priority over optimisation
- Can break LinkedIn "rules" deliberately (e.g., longer paragraphs, no hashtags)
- The voice should sound unmistakably like a real person wrote it
- Total length: flexible, but typically 200-400 words

**What makes it different from the LinkedIn-optimised version:**

- More personal vulnerability and reflection
- Less "engineered" feeling — no visible structure tricks
- The CTA is softer or absent — it's more about resonance than engagement metrics
- May use more complex sentence structures for emotional texture
- Fewer (or no) hashtags

## 4. Post Formatting Rules

- **Line breaks matter**: Every new idea gets a new line. Dense paragraphs kill posts on LinkedIn.
- **First 2 lines are everything**: They determine whether anyone reads the rest. This is your hook.
- **"See more" placement**: LinkedIn cuts off after roughly 2-3 lines. Everything above that fold must create enough curiosity to earn the click.
- **Short sentences build pace**: Especially in the opening. One idea per sentence. Fragments are fine.
- **Em-dashes over commas**: They create a more conversational, editorial feel.
- **Numbers over words**: "3 clients" not "three clients". Numbers stop the eye.
- **No emojis in every paragraph**: One or two per post maximum, if at all. Use them for emphasis or a single visual break — not decoration.
- **Hashtags at the end**: 3-5 relevant hashtags. Not inline. Not at the top.
- **CTA should feel natural**: Not "Drop a comment below!" but something that invites genuine conversation or shares.

## 5. Visual Guidance Summary

While this skill focuses on the written post, here are visual pairing recommendations to include in your output notes:

- **Thematic / build in public posts**: 3-4 photo collage showing different moments from the story. Authentic, not staged.
- **Thought leadership / educational**: Bold statement graphics, infographics, or authoritative photos (speaking on stage, working with clients).
- **Situational storytelling**: Single photo that mirrors the emotion of the hook. Your facial expression should match the energy of the opening line.
- **No image (for maximum shares)**: If the post makes a universal, highly shareable point with a strong villain/opinion, consider posting text-only. People are more likely to repost text than someone else's face.

## 6. Ideation Strategies

When extracting post ideas from meeting notes or topic briefs:

1. **Look for the story**: What moment, conversation, or discovery carries emotional weight?
2. **Find the villain**: What frustration, broken system, or bad advice can you call out?
3. **Spot the transformation**: Where is the before/after? The contrast?
4. **Identify the unexpected detail**: What's the surprising stat, quote, or fact that would make someone stop scrolling?
5. **Check for universality**: Will people beyond this specific industry or company relate to the theme?
6. **Extract the lesson**: What's the one takeaway that helps someone else?
7. **Find the human**: Names, characters, real people — stories about humans outperform stories about companies.
8. **Note the specifics**: Dates, numbers, locations, job titles, team sizes — these details make posts believable.

## 7. Anti-AI Writing Rules (CRITICAL)

This is the most important section. If the post sounds like AI wrote it, nothing else matters — not the hook, not the structure, not the storytelling. Readers increasingly recognise AI-generated prose and it destroys trust instantly.

### The Root Problem

AI writing sounds like AI writing because it reaches for the statistically common option every time. It doesn't vary. It doesn't know that the third "something shifted" in a post has lost all power. It just keeps generating the highest-probability completion.

The actual tell is not any single word or phrase — it's the **density of defaults**. When the same constructions, the same vague phrases, the same rhythm patterns appear repeatedly, the prose flattens. It stops feeling like a specific person wrote it and starts feeling assembled from interchangeable parts.

### The Accumulation Principle

No single banned element makes prose bad. One em-dash is fine. One "And" sentence-start is fine. The problem is accumulation — when the same patterns appear reflexively, repeatedly, and interchangeably. Watch for density, not individual instances.

### Banned Constructions for LinkedIn Posts — HARD RULES

These are ZERO-TOLERANCE rules. Not guidelines. Not "use sparingly." Zero instances per post.

**HARD RULE 1 — ZERO negative parallelism / "Didn't X, did Y":**
All forms are banned:

- "Not X, but Y" / "Not just X — Y"
- "It wasn't about X. It was about Y." / "It's not the X. It's the Y."
- "We didn't build X. We built Y." / "I didn't X. I Y'd."
- "Not replacement. Partnership." / "Not a feature. A medium."
- "Not because X. But because Y."
  ZERO instances per post. Even if a client's direct quote uses this pattern, paraphrase it into a positive statement. Say what it IS without first saying what it ISN'T.
- BAD: "We didn't build a feature. We built a new creative medium."
- GOOD: "We built a new creative medium — and people started using it in ways we never planned for."

**HARD RULE 2 — ZERO triple-beat constructions:**
Three parallel items stacked for rhythmic emphasis. All forms banned:

- Three fragment sentences: "No gaming engine. No 3D modeling. Just words." ← BANNED
- Triple "Some/They" pattern: "Some were architects. Some were game designers. Some were curious." ← BANNED
- Three noun fragments: "The depth. The specificity. The realness." ← BANNED
- Tripled list after a setup: "No paid marketing. Just Discord, word of mouth, people experimenting." ← BANNED
- Three escalating restatements: "They're staying. They're building. They're creating." ← BANNED
  INSTEAD: Write a flowing sentence. "They were architects, game designers, and people who'd never touched 3D software before" — one sentence, same information, no artificial rhythm.

**HARD RULE 3 — ZERO echo-line poetics:**
Restating the same idea in slightly different words on consecutive lines:

- BAD: "Users aren't just trying it. They're staying. They're building with it." (three lines saying "users are retained")
- BAD: "That's the actual magic. That's when you know you've built something real." (two "That's" sentences saying the same thing)
- BAD: "That's not growth. That's people solving problems." (restatement dressed as contrast)
  INSTEAD: Say it once with specifics and move on. Trust single statements to carry weight.

**HARD RULE 4 — ZERO grand summative statements:**

- "That's what this milestone actually means." ← BANNED
- "And that changes everything." ← BANNED
- "That's the whole thing, right there." ← BANNED
- "That sentence. That's why we do this." ← BANNED
  INSTEAD: End on a concrete detail, a specific next step, or a genuine question. Not a pronouncement.

**HARD RULE 5 — ZERO present-participle trailing clauses:**
Tacking ", [verb]-ing [consequence]" onto the end of sentences to make vague claims about significance:

- "A team of researchers across 12 countries, publishing their first paper from it." ← BANNED
- "...demonstrating the power of community-driven innovation." ← BANNED
- "...reflecting a broader shift in how people work." ← BANNED
- "...emphasizing the importance of accessible tools." ← BANNED
  INSTEAD: Make it its own sentence or cut it. "Their first paper came out of that shared workspace." is better than the trailing participle.

**HARD RULE 6 — Zero em-dashes:**
Em-dashes are a known AI tell. LLMs reach for them constantly, and readers notice. Use commas, full stops, colons, or parentheses instead. Zero em-dashes allowed, anywhere in the post including hooks.

**Also banned (use-sparingly rules — max 1 per post):**

**Sequential action pairs**: "X, then Y" as a mechanical rhythm.

- Vary sentence structure. Not every sequence needs "then."

**Misapplied epic tone / Undue notability emphasis**: Treating routine business milestones as if they were world-historical events. AI can't resist explaining why everything matters.

- BAD: "This changes everything." / "a pivotal moment" / "a broader movement"
- BAD: "This represents a fundamental shift in..." / "marking a new era of..."
- Scale the emotional register to match the actual stakes. Not everything is historic.

### Banned Vocabulary

These words and phrases are the most common AI tells in LinkedIn content. Avoid them entirely:

**Overused abstract nouns**: tapestry, landscape, interplay, intricacies, nuance/nuanced, multifaceted, dynamics, framework, paradigm

**Overused verbs**: delve, foster, garner, underscore, showcase, highlight, emphasize, reflect, align, enhance, navigate, unpack, bolster/bolstered

**Overused adjectives**: pivotal, crucial, vital, vibrant, intricate, nuanced, multifaceted, profound, compelling, poignant, evocative, visceral, palpable, meticulous, enduring, valuable, scenic, breathtaking

**Overused adverbs**: seemingly, arguably, notably, importantly, ultimately, fundamentally, inherently, undeniably, understandably

**Legacy/importance puffery**: "stands as a testament to", "a legacy of [noun]", "lasting impact", "enduring [noun]", "nestled", "boasts", "rich tapestry of", "breathtaking"

**Promotional/brochure language**: "offers a unique blend of", "promises to [verb]", "serves as a [metaphor]", "provides a platform for", "reflects a commitment to"

**Vague emotion placeholders**: "something shifted", "something in [his/her] [body part] [verb]", "the weight of", "the rawness of", "a need he/she couldn't name"

**Default transition phrases**: "for a moment", "finally", "and then, something changed", "it was then that"

### The Self-Edit Protocol (MANDATORY — run before presenting output)

Go through the post line by line. For each line, check:

1. **"Didn't X / Did Y" scan**: Does this line contain ANY form of "not X but Y", "didn't X, did Y", "it's not about X, it's about Y"? If yes → rewrite as a direct positive statement. Target: ZERO instances.

2. **Triple-beat scan**: Are there three parallel fragments, three items in "Some X. Some Y. Some Z." format, or three escalating restatements? If yes → combine into one flowing sentence. Target: ZERO instances.

3. **Echo-line scan**: Does this line say the same thing as the previous line in different words? If yes → delete one of them. Target: ZERO instances.

4. **Summative statement scan**: Does this line announce what the post means ("That's what this is about", "And that changes everything")? If yes → replace with a specific detail or cut entirely. Target: ZERO instances.

5. **Interchangeability test**: Could this sentence appear unchanged in a different post about a different company? If yes → rewrite with details specific to THIS story.

6. **Vocabulary scan**: Does this line contain any word from the banned AI vocabulary list? If yes → replace with a specific, grounded alternative.

If the post has ANY violations of rules 1-4, fix them before presenting. These are hard failures, not soft warnings.

### What To Do Instead

**Instead of negative parallelism** → State what it IS directly. "The 100 million means something because of Keiko's tracker, the Kyoto bakery, the climate researchers." No need to first say what it ISN'T.

**Instead of echo-line poetics** → If the second line doesn't add new information, cut it. Trust single statements to carry weight.

**Instead of triple-beat fragments** → Write a full sentence. "What struck me was how different each use case was — a bakery, a research team, a teenager — and how none of them used Notion the way we expected."

**Instead of vague emotion words** → Name the specific feeling or show the specific thought. Not "something shifted" but "I stopped reading the dashboard and started reading the stories."

**Instead of grand summative statements** → End on a specific detail or a genuine question. Not "That's the whole thing, right there" but the actual detail that moved you.

### The Operating Principle

Every banned element is a shortcut that prevents the harder, better work of: figuring out what's actually happening, understanding how this specific person would say it, building meaning through specifics rather than announcing it through rhetoric, and trusting the reader to feel what's been earned rather than being told what to feel.
