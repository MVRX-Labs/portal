# Style and Process Guide Template

Every client gets a Style and Process Guide before a single post goes live.
It's the single source of truth for voice, structure and strategy.

This document walks through each section, what it should contain and why it matters.

---

## 1. Audience

Define exactly who the client is trying to reach. Not "everyone." Not "business owners." Get specific.

Two to three audience segments, each described in plain language:

- **Who they are** (role, seniority, industry)
- **What they care about** (problems, goals, aspirations)
- **Where they are in the buying cycle** (awareness, consideration, decision)

The more specific the audience, the sharper the content.

A post that speaks to everyone speaks to no one.

---

## 2. Brand Context

Capture what the client's company actually does and how it positions itself.

Cover:

- **What the company does** in one to two sentences, free of jargon
- **Core product or service** and the problem it solves
- **Market positioning** -- who are they compared to, and what makes them different
- **Stage and size** -- startup, scale-up, enterprise; this affects tone
- **Any relevant brand history** that shapes how they show up online

This section gives the copywriter enough context to write with authority without needing to be an insider.

---

## 3. Key People

Name the person (or people) whose LinkedIn profile this content will be published under.

For each person, include:

- **Name and role**
- **Background** -- what they're known for, what they've built, where they've worked
- **Public persona** -- how they already come across online (formal, casual, provocative, understated)
- **Content involvement** -- do they draft ideas, approve final copy, or fully delegate?

The content has to sound like it came from this person's brain, not a marketing department.

---

## 4. Voice and Tone

This is the personality of every post. It should feel consistent enough that a reader could identify the author without seeing the name.

Define:

- **Core tone** (e.g. direct and dry, warm and conversational, provocative and contrarian)
- **Language register** -- British English or American English, formal or informal
- **What to lean into** -- confidence, vulnerability, humour, authority, storytelling
- **What to avoid** -- corporate speak, hedging, overselling, excessive enthusiasm

Include two to three short "this, not that" examples:

| Do this                          | Not this                                                                  |
| -------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "We shipped it in three days."   | "We were delighted to announce the launch of our latest solution."        |
| "Most teams get this wrong."     | "It's worth noting that many organisations face challenges in this area." |
| "Here's what actually happened." | "I'd like to share some key takeaways from a recent experience."          |

The voice section is non-negotiable. Without it, every post risks sounding generic.

---

## 5. Content Themes and Pillars

List three to five recurring topics the client's posts should orbit around.

Each theme should include:

- **Theme name** (short label)
- **What it covers** (one to two sentences)
- **Why it matters to the audience** (connects back to Section 1)
- **Example angle** (a specific post idea that fits this theme)

Example structure:

> **Theme: Hiring Without a Playbook**
> Covers the reality of building teams in early-stage companies where there's no HR function, no structured interview process and no employer brand.
> Matters because the audience (founders, 10-50 headcount) is hiring constantly and getting it wrong.
> Angle: "I've hired 30 people in 18 months. Here are the 3 questions I ask every single one."

Themes keep the content focused. Without them, the feed turns into random observations.

---

## 6. Style Options

Most clients won't use the same post style every time. Define two to three styles they'll rotate between.

Common options:

- **Classic LinkedIn** -- short, sharp sentences. White space between every line. Designed for skimming on mobile. Best for opinion-led and insight-driven posts.
- **Short paragraphs** -- slightly denser. Two to three sentence paragraphs with a narrative arc. Better for storytelling and case studies.
- **Super short** -- a single provocative question or statement. Five lines maximum. High engagement, low depth. Best used sparingly.

For each style, note when to use it and how often (e.g. "Classic LinkedIn for 3 in 5 posts, Short paragraphs for storytelling, Super short once a fortnight").

---

## 7. Post Structure

Every post follows a skeleton. Define the default structure here.

**Hook** (first two to three lines, visible before "see more")

- The hook decides whether anyone reads the rest
- Should spark curiosity, challenge an assumption or promise a specific payoff
- Write three hook options per post, pick the strongest
- Separate each hook line with a blank line for visual breathing room

**Body** (the substance)

- Tell one story, make one point, share one insight
- Use the chosen style from Section 6
- Include a twist, a counterintuitive finding or a moment of tension
- Keep total post length between 900 and 1,500 characters

**CTA** (the close)

- End with a clear ask: a question, a repost prompt, a follow nudge
- Keep it to one CTA per post -- don't stack them
- Match the CTA to the goal (engagement, follows, DMs, link clicks)

Example CTA formats:

- "What's your take? Drop it below."
- "Repost if you've seen this play out in your team."
- "Follow [Name] for more on [topic]."

---

## 8. Image and Visual Guidelines

At least one in every two posts should include an image.

For each post, decide which visual category fits:

- **Thematic / build in public** -- photo collage (3-4 images) showing the client in action, behind the scenes, real moments
- **Thought leadership** -- branded graphics, infographics, bold statements with consistent fonts and colours
- **Situational storytelling** -- single photo matching the emotion of the hook
- **No image** -- text-only for maximum shareability on universal topics

Key rules:

- Crop to 4:5 ratio to maximise screen space
- Client should be front and centre in any photo (75-85% of frame)
- Natural lighting, no heavy filters
- Build a rolling photo bank so visuals never hold up publishing

---

## 9. Posting Cadence and Timing

Define the rhythm:

- **Posts per week** (3-5 is standard for active profiles)
- **Preferred days** (weekdays tend to outperform weekends for B2B)
- **Preferred times** (text-only: 10am-12pm, text+image: 8am-10:30am)
- **Content calendar cycle** -- weekly batching, fortnightly review, or rolling drafts

Also note:

- Who approves posts before publishing
- Lead time required for approval
- How far in advance posts should be drafted

---

## 10. Client-Specific Notes

Catch anything that doesn't fit neatly into the sections above.

This might include:

- Specific phrases or words the client likes to use (or hates)
- Competitors they do not want to mention
- Upcoming launches, events or milestones to weave into content
- Preferred hashtag strategy (if any -- generally avoid)
- Any existing brand guidelines or tone documents to reference
- Tags they regularly want included (people, companies)

---

## Checklist Before Finalising

Before this guide is handed over, confirm:

- [ ] Audience is specific enough to write a post to, not just about
- [ ] Voice section has concrete "this, not that" examples
- [ ] At least three content themes are defined with example angles
- [ ] Post structure is clear enough that a new writer could follow it
- [ ] Image guidelines match the client's existing visual presence
- [ ] Cadence and approval process are agreed with the client
- [ ] Client has reviewed and signed off on the guide
