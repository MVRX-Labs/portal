# LinkedIn Post Visual Guidance: How to Use Visuals to Boost Impact

Your imagery should match the tone and purpose of your post, not just fill space. The image has as much impact on your post performance as your first two written lines.

## Ask Yourself 3 Questions

1. Do the visuals on this post help scaffold my message, make it more believable and prove authority?
2. Do the visuals illustrate or complement the story I am telling? Is the text and visual aligned?
3. Do the visuals enhance the type of personal brand I'm building? Is this how I want to be seen, judged and perceived by future fans, followers and customers?

Don't let imagery be an afterthought.

There are 4 types of visual styles you can choose (excluding video and carousels):

1. Thematic posts / build in public storytelling
2. Thought leadership / educational
3. Personal and situational storytelling
4. No image

---

## 01: Thematic / Build in Public Storytelling

### Goal

Visually illustrate your story. These types of visuals should be unique to you - give people an insider look into your world. They should convey your 'busyness' and 'business' and represent you 'in action' - so people feel like they're part of your journey - both personally and professionally.

### Action

Upload a 3-4x photo collage. 3-4 separate images that capture different moments or moods from your hook/post. (Any more than 4 and the 5th won't display unless readers click the '+' button - so chances are very few people will see the 5th). Pick high-quality images and crop any 'dead space'.

If it's a post on health, you can upload 3-4 pictures that depict health in different ways (e.g. you at the gym, a healthy salad, you walking your dogs, etc). If your post is a 'week in the life' post, you could have pictures of: a coffee at your desk (morning ritual), you on a train (commuting to work), you meeting a client / at an event and your laptop on your website/landing page.

**Tip:** Upload the 4 images separately so you can reorder/preview how they crop. The first image must be the main "hero" shot - ideally of you! Each photo is a comment trigger - a chance to spark discussion. Ensure every photo is different, but all are aligned and add to the story/message you're conveying.

## 02: Thought Leadership / Educational

### Goal

Reinforce your credibility and authority as a service provider. Your visuals must match your target customer and offer. If you're selling to retired business owners buying yachts, a photo in your PJs with a cuppa won't cut it. But if you run a community for remote workers, casual home or cafe shots and Zoom co-working screenshots make sense. Bottom line: premium offers and pricing must be backed by premium aesthetics. You want to be 'approachable' but also aspirational.

### Action

You can either upload:

- **No face imagery** - bold statements, quotes or visual infographics that convey a message, break down a concept or strikingly provide digestible bite-sized value. Ideally, these should be uniquely branded (to match your signature style e.g. consistent fonts, colours, iconography).
- **Face included 'authoritative' photos** - you speaking on stage, in a professional setting, or working with clients to convey you 'in action' doing the work you say you do. Think: fewer 'selfies' and more professional shots, stylised to match the 'persona' your target audience would buy from.

---

## 03: Situational Storytelling

### Goal

Visually illustrate one specific story (rooted in time, place and character). This visual should be used in posts where you want to convey a specific emotion (to discuss something contextual and situational). Ideally, when you're making one singular point / talking about one singular scenario.

### Action

Upload one single photo of you that mirrors the emotion or tone of your hook.

- Post hook: "I can't believe this just happened..." -> Photo: You looking shocked, wide-eyed, or amused.

You can also impose a screenshot over your photo (using Canva) to give extra detail. Screenshots of messages or emails work well - because they tease some 'drama'.

**Tip:** Think of your image as a thumbnail for your post. Your facial expression should grab attention and match the energy of your opening line (shock, disbelief, excitement, etc.). It should feel as if you've just written this story on your phone, taken a photo of yourself in the moment and shared it online (rather than a rehearsed, stylised and highly edited post). This makes your story original and believable!

---

## 04: No Visual

### Goal

Share a universal message that has a strong villain or callout that affects - or is at least accessible to - a mass audience. Think broad themes like 'leadership', 'mental health', 'working culture' - themes that affect all humans day to day and trigger some form of reaction, emotion or opinion. Help people get to know your unique POV, opinions and perspective and get as many SHARES as possible.

### Action

Write your short-form post. It must be digestible content that makes one clear point about one clear topic in a strong way. Easy to read, understand and agree with. Share it without an image.

**Why no image?** Because it maximises shareability. People are *less likely* to repost something if it has a photo of a person they do not know (i.e. of a stranger). But if it's a strong message they agree with, it's an easy repost.

## 5 Quick Tips to Succeed

1. **Match tone to visuals.** If you're telling a super emotive personal story, a highly stylised photo of you where you are clearly posing in a studio doesn't make sense. It takes away from the rawness of your messaging and comes across as inauthentic. This is why pairing visuals strategically is so important.

2. **Build a photo bank.** Get into the habit of taking pictures daily, as you're about and about living life and conducting business. This means you can collect a bank of photos and then pair them with posts once you write them. It saves a lot of time if you have shots in different outfits, locations, settings, etc. all in one place.

3. **Crop out dead space.** In pictures of you, ensure you are front and centre. If the photo is taken from a distance and you only take up 20% of it, crop out the edges so you occupy at least 75-85% of the photo. You want the focus to be on you, not the floor, sky or space around you.

4. **Maximise natural lighting.** It helps your photos look better quality and more flattering. Avoid harsh overhead lighting, heavy filters, smoothing, or colour grading.

5. **Preserve image quality.** If taking photos on a mobile phone, either airdrop them to yourself or put them in a Google Drive folder from your phone that you can access via desktop. Don't use WhatsApp or social media apps to send them to yourself from your phone (most of these apps reduce resolution quality).
